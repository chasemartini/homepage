A Couple Useful Apps

August 22nd, 2019

I really love discovering new apps and games on my mobile, so here are a couple I use that are worth mentioning:

10000+ (Hours Tracker)

This app is what I use to log my hours and keep track of my progress. It's fairly basic, but it's actually very much in line with the goal of this site to see how close I am to achieving my 10,000 hour "master" goal. It tells you what days you have logged hours, the trend of how long you are averaging daily, and also estimates how long it will take you to reach 10,000 hours if you keep on your current pace. When I first started and was putting in a lot of hours every day, it estimated that it would take me about 10 years to acheive my target, but due to slowing down, it now estimates that it will take me 20 years. However,once I start studying full-time and eventually get a dev job, this estimate will decrease greatly.

Beginning Programming for Dummies

May, 29th 2019

I ordered this book, Beginning Programming for Dummies, and have started reading the first section, "Getting Started." There is quite a lot of information on the history of programming and general points on how computers and programming work.

One of the most interesting things I have learned so far is that the first designs for a programmable machine came from Charles Babbage all the way back in 1834. He proposed a mechanical, steam-powered machine called the Analytical Engine. By feeding punch cards into the machine, it could solve different problems. Then, a woman named Ada Lovelace wrote a program for this machine to calculate and print the Bernoulli numbers, essentially becomming the world's first computer programmer.

More than 100 years later in 1943, the US Army funded the creation of the first true computer to calculate artillery trajectories, called ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer). This one required the physical actions of flipping switches and rearranging cables to program it.

What is Your Age in Dog Years?

March 14th, 2019

So, this is my first blog post! One of many to come! At this point, I have logged just over 60 hours of coding, learning HTML5, CSS3, and a little bit of JavaScript. I am using Codecademy to learn the initial ideas, and then I am using FreeCodeCamp to go back over what I have learned, taking lots of notes,and then when I feel comfortable, applying them to my webpage here. I have been finding so far that although these sources are quite similar, they cover slightly different aspects and are very complementary when used together. Other sources I have found useful have been the numerous tutorials you can find through YouTube.

The first thing I would like to point you to, so that you can see some programming in action, is the dog years calculator I created at the bottom of this post. Just underneath this entry, you will find a button, that when you click it, my page will ask you for your age. When you enter your age, it will determine how old you are in dog years. At our home, we have 3 cats and 1 dog, so it's quite interesting to realize the value of their life by figuring out how old you would be if you were in their shoes!

So, the way I created the function that calculates your age in dog years, is basically challenging the current misconception that every dog year equals 7 years in human years. The actual life span and aging of a dog dictates that the first 2 years of a dog's life is equal to 10.5 years each. This means that by the time your dog is 2 years old, they are 21 years old in respective terms. However, after these first 2 years, aging slows to 4 years per human year. Therefore the calculation is a little more complicated. So, I have made it easy for you to see just how many years you would have lived through if you were a dog.

Cool, right? :-)
